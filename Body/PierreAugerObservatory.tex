\chapter{The Pierre Auger Observatory}\label{chap:auger}

The Pierre Auger Observatory is located near the town of Malarg\"ue in the Mendoza province of Argentina between the Andes mountain range and la Pampa Amarilla.
Auger, as the observatory is commonly referred to, is the world's largest cosmic-ray observatory, covering a total area of \qty{3000}{\kilo\meter^{2}}.
In context, this is about the size of the state of Rhode Island, or for those with a more worldly perspective, slightly larger than the size of Luxembourg.
The observatory's size allows the detection of ultra-high-energy cosmic rays, starting at energies of $\approx$\qty[parse-numbers=false]{10^{17}}{\electronvolt}, with high statistics.
However, the observatory is ground based and therefore indirectly detects the highest energy particles in the known Universe from the air showers they initiate after colliding with atmospheric nuclei.

Not only is Auger the world's largest cosmic-ray observatory, but adding to the novelty is the hybrid detection mode employed by the observatory.
The hybrid design allows simultaneous air-shower observations with two separate, yet complimentary techniques.
The observatory consists of two primary detectors, the surface detector (SD), an array of over 1600 water-Cherenkov detectors (WCDs) spread across the full \qty{3000}{\kilo\meter^{2}} footprint of the observatory, and a fluorescence detector (FD), 24 fluorescence telescopes overlooking the SD array at four locations around the edges of the array.
Auger has been recording data in hybrid mode for over 20 years, officially starting in 2004 and with full completion of the SD in 2008.
In March 2026, Auger finished deploying detectors for the next-generation upgrade of the observatory, AugerPrime.
An international funding agreement will keep the observatory in operation until at least 2035, allowing for high-quality air-shower measurements with AugerPrime, and the potential for prototyping detectors for future experiments in the Argentinian Pampa.
This chapter will describe the Pierre Auger Observatory and its detectors systems in detail.


\section{Surface Detector}\label{sec:sd}

The Pierre Auger Observatory SD consists of 1660 WCDs deployed on a triangular grid to cover the full surface area of the array.
The spacing of the grid is \qty{1500}{\meter}, designed such that all cosmic rays landing within the array grid and above an energy of \qty[parse-numbers=false]{10^{18.5}}{\electronvolt} are detected.
Within the SD-1500 are two nested triangular sub-arrays of WCDs with denser spacing to lower the energy threshold of cosmic-ray detection.
They are the SD-750, with \qty{750}{\meter} spacing between each detector and the SD-433, nested within the SD-750 and with a detector spacing of \qty{433}{\meter}.
The SD-750 consists of 61 WCDs covering an area of \qty{23.5}{\kilo\meter^{2}}, lowering the energy detection threshold to \qty[parse-numbers=false]{10^{17.5}}{\electronvolt}, while the SD-433 consists of 19 WCDs over an area of \qty{1.95}{\kilo\meter^{2}} and can detect air showers as low as \qty[parse-numbers=false]{10^{16.5}}{\electronvolt} in energy.
A map of the Pierre Auger Observatory, including all three SD arrays, is shown in INSERTFIGURE.

Each WCD is a cylinder of \qty{1.2}{\meter} height and \qty{3.6}{\meter} diameter filled with \qty{11000}{\liter} of ultra-pure water.
The water inside the WCD is contained in a bladder composed of Tyvek with a fully reflective inner surface.
At the top of each WCD are three, equally spaced, 9-inch phototmultiplier tubes (PMTs) to detect the Cherenkov radiation generated from ultra-relativistic secondary particles from cosmic-ray air showers traversing the ultra-pure water.
The PMT signals are digitized by a \qty{40}{\mega\hertz} flash analog-to-digital converter (FADC), corresponding to a bin width of \qty{25}{\nano\second} for the FADC conversion.
Two signals are digitized per PMT, the low-gain channel, taken directly from the PMT anode, and the high-gain channel, taken from the last PMT dynode with a nominal amplification of 32 times the anode channel, providing a total dynamic range of approximately 17 bits.
All processes are handled by the Auger Unified Board (UB), the main electronics board of each SD station and is described in more detail at REFERENCE.
Other components of the individual WCDs include a standalone communications system, for communications and data transfer to the Central Data Acquisition System (CDAS) of the observatory, and a solar power system for powering the station.
The solar power system allows an approximate 100\% uptime of the SD.

Signals from the PMTs are digitized in electronics units, integrated ADC units where the integration is performed over the bin width of the FADC.
The digitized signals are converted to units of Vertical Equivalent Muons (VEM) to provide a common reference unit across the array, where one VEM is defined as the total signal deposited in the WCD by a single muon traversing the WCD vertically and centrally.
The conversion to VEM is performed ``online'' (within the electronics of the station) at each WCD using the calibration histograms of the individual PMTs.
A WCD equipped with an RDC hodoscope was used to experimentally confirm the conversion (REF 6 Calibration).
The calibration histograms are generated from \qty{60}{\second} of atmospheric muon data recorded with the \texttt{muon\_buffer} of the local station.
See~\cref{app:layered_wcd} for further discussion of the \texttt{muon\_buffer}.

\subsection{SD Triggers}\label{sec:sd_triggers}

The atmospheric muons recorded for local station calibration arrive at an approximate rate of \qty{3}{\kilo\hertz}.
A tiered SD trigger system reduces this to a feasible rate for physics analyses.
To understand the threshold levels defined for the first two tiers of the trigger system an understanding of the peak and charge calibration histograms is needed.
The charge histogram is generated by integrating the total signals of individual atmospheric muons triggering the \texttt{muon\_buffer}, with charges ($Q$) measured in units of time-integrated FADC bins, or simply FADC bins.
The second maximum in the charge histogram is known as the ``muon peak'', where the charge value of this maximum, represented as $Q_{\rm VEM}^{\rm peak}$, defines the conversion constant from electronics units to VEM units.
Related to the charge histogram is the peak histogram, generated from the maximum amplitudes of the signal recorded by the \texttt{muon\_buffer}.
The peak histogram has units of FADC bins per \qty{25}{\nano\second} and is sometimes referred to as the current ($I$) histogram because the second maximum of this histogram, represented as $I_{\rm VEM}^{\rm peak}$, defines the end-to-end gain of the PMTs.
The end-to-end PMT gains are set individually and a local station-level calibration is done to continually adjust this value (see AUGER CALIBRATION SD for more information).

The first-level (T1) and second-level (T2) triggers are generated locally by the station electronics and depend directly on $I_{\rm VEM}^{\rm peak}$.
Two types of T1 trigger are possible, each designed for sensitivity to different particle types.
The first is a simple threshold trigger, Th-T1, requiring a coincidence of all three PMTs to measure a signal above 1.75$\,I_{\rm VEM}^{\rm peak}$.
This reduces the trigger rate of the SD from $\approx$\qty{3}{\kilo\hertz} to $\approx$\qty{100}{\hertz} and is more sensitive to muons, as these particles generate large amplitude and temporally quick signals in the PMTs.
The second T1 trigger is a time-over-threshold trigger, ToT-T1, requiring two PMTs to coincidentally measure above 0.2$\,I_{\rm VEM}^{\rm peak}$ in at least 13 FADC bins (\qty{325}{\nano\second}) over a 120 FADC bin (\qty{3}{\micro\second}) sliding window.
The ToT-T1 trigger reduces the trigger rate to $\approx$\qty{2}{\hertz} and is more sensitive to electromagnetic particles, as the Cherenkov light generated by these particles in the WCD is spread in time.
ToT triggers are automatically promoted to T2 triggers, whereas the Th-T1 trigger is only promoted to a Th-T2 if all three PMTs measure in coincidence a signal above 3.2$\,I_{\rm VEM}^{\rm peak}$.
The Th-T2 further reduces the trigger rate from \qty{100}{\hertz} to \qty{20}{hertz}.
Over the 20 years of Auger data taking, PMT failures have occurred in individual WCDs, with these PMTs referred to as ``masked'' PMTs.
In these conditions, the thresholds for Th and ToT triggers change to the values described in AUGER TRIGGER AND APERTURE, as a three PMT coincidence is no longer viable.

All station level T2 triggers are transferred to CDAS, where the T3 trigger is formed from a time and space coincidence of stations with a T2 trigger.
Once a T3 trigger is formed, a T3 request is sent to all stations participating in the T3 trigger and all nearby stations.
The data for each station participating in the T3 request is sent to CDAS where it is stored for further physics analyses.
The T4 trigger, the first physics trigger of the SD, requires stricter time and space coincidence of the stations than those used in the T3 trigger.
Stricter coincidence conditions ensure the events passing the T4 trigger are true physics events rather than random coincident triggering of nearby stations.
The T5 trigger, the final SD trigger, applies a fiducial quality cut to the true air-shower events.
A common fiducial cut to use is a 6T5 cut, also known as a 6T5 trigger, which requires the station with the highest signal in an event to be fully surrounded by six working stations at the time of the event.
This cut selects events fully contained within the SD array to ensure they have a reliable SD reconstruction and is a common cut to use for high-level SD analyses.

\section{Fluorescence Detector}\label{sec:fd}

The Fluorescence Detector (FD) of the Pierre Auger Observatory consists of 24 fluorescence telescopes to observe the longitudinal development of cosmic-ray air showers above approximately \qty[parse-numbers=false]{10^{18}}{\electronvolt} in energy.
As an air shower develops, the ultra-relativistic charged secondary particles will excite nitrogen molecules in Earth's atmosphere, which, upon de-excitation, generate fluorescence light measurable by the fluorescence telescopes.
The telescopes are split between four locations surrounding the SD array: Los Leones, Los Morados, Loma Amarilla, and Coihueco.
Each location hosts six fluorescence telescopes covering a combined \qty{180}{\degree} azimuthal field of view overlooking the SD array, with individual telescopes providing an azimuthal and elevation field of view of \qty{30}{\degree}$\,\times\,$\qty{30}{\degree}.
Operation of the telescopes is possible only on clear, moonless nights, without rain or strong winds, limiting the uptime of the FD to approximately 15\%.

The fluorescence telescopes follow a Schmidt optics design to minimize coma aberrations, arising from incoming light rays not being parallel to the axis of the mirror.
The basics of the telescope system consist of a spherical aperture with a UV filter and Schmidt corrector ring, a segmented mirror, and a camera of PMTs.
Fluorescence light enters the aperture with \qty{2.2}{\meter} diameter where it is filtered to UV wavelengths to reduce the amount of background light reaching the camera.
The filtered light is collected by a segmented mirror of nearly \qty{13}{\meter^{2}} in size and focused to the telescope's camera, composed of 440 total PMTs arranged in a 22$\,\times\,$20 grid.
The camera PMTs of each telescope connect to front-end electronics boards that handle the digitization of the PMT signals and the first and second level hardware triggers of the FD telescope. 
A schematic of a single fluorescence telescope is shown in FIGURE.

Three additional fluorescence telescopes, known as the High Elevation Auger Telescopes (HEAT), were installed next to the Coihueco FD site in 2009.
HEAT is similar to the other fluorescence telescopes, but the main difference is each telescope of HEAT can observe in either an upwards or downwards configuration.
In the upwards configuration, the telescopes of HEAT are pointed upwards by \qty{29}{\degree} in elevation, providing an elevation field of view of approximately \qtyrange{30}{58}{\degree}.
The downwards configuration has the same field of view in elevation as the other 24 fluorescence telescopes.
Elevating the telescope field of view allows measurements of the longitudinal development of lower energy air showers that reach their maximum development at higher altitudes, with HEAT lowering the fluorescence detection threshold to approximately \qty[parse-numbers=false]{10^{17}}{\electronvolt} in energy.
The HEAT field of view overlooks the SD-750 and SD-433 arrays, delivering high-quality hybrid detections of air showers at energies near and between the second knee and ankle of the cosmic ray energy spectrum.

\section{Event Reconstruction}\label{sec:reconstruction}

The event reconstruction for the Pierre Auger Observatory occurs separately for the SD and FD, and is later combined for hybrid air-shower analyses.
All reconstruction occurs within Offline, the observatory's software package for analyzing air-shower data.
For the SD reconstruction, two main measurements are needed for each station that recorded event data: the start time of the signal in the station, to determine the geometry of the arriving cosmic-ray air shower, and the total signal in the station's PMTs, to determine an event-level observable related to the air-shower energy.
Determination of the time range of the event in the station and the conversion of the PMT signals from FADC units to VEM must be done before analyzing the station data for event-level analyses.
The time range is determined using the procedure described in REF2020JINST, and the conversion of FADC to VEM is performed using the SD calibration histograms described in~\cref{sec:sd_triggers}, with further information on the conversion provided in REF.

Signal time windows are determined on a PMT-to-PMT basis.
Therefore, the nominal start and stop times for the station are taken from the station PMT with the longest signal time window.
The station's PMTs are averaged bin-by-bin, where the averaged PMT signal will be integrated over the nominal time window of the signal to determine the total signal measured in VEM within the SD station.
When the station is close to the air-shower axis, large signals are measured in the tank which can saturate either the high-gain, low-gain, or both channels of the PMT.
The saturation is dependent on both the distance to the air-shower axis and the energy of the arriving shower (REF 2020JINST).
In the case of no PMT saturation, the high-gain PMT channels are used to determine the total measured PMT signal, whereas in the case of high-gain saturation, the low-gain PMT channels are used instead.
In the case of both high-gain and low-gain saturation, the station is labeled as a saturated station.
Software within the Offline framework is used to recover the signals of such saturated stations for analysis (REF GAP2008-030, Darko ICRC2013).
However, identifying the signal window of the individual PMTs is always done with the high-gain PMT channel.

The air-shower geometry (point of impact on the ground and arrival direction) are reconstructed from an iterative procedure involving both the signal start times and total signals of the SD stations participating in the event.
A signal-weighted barycenter, determined from the center-of-mass of stations in the event, is used as the first estimate of the impact point on ground.
Assuming speed of light propagation of the particles, a plane wave fit to the start times of the three stations with the largest signal closest to the barycenter determines the first estimate of the arrival direction.
All stations with start times outside of the range \qtyrange{-2}{+1}{\micro\second} from the arrival time determined by the fit are labeled as accidental stations, stations that do not participate in the event yet were triggered, likely from accidental muons.
Stations participating in the event are fit using a chi-squared minimization process to a timing model of the wavefront arrival that accounts for wavefront curvature.
Four values are fit: the directional cosines, $u$ and $v$, of the unit vector describing the shower arrival direction (i.e. $\hat{a} = u\,\hat{x} + v\,\hat{y} + \sqrt{1 - u^{2} - v^{2}}\,\hat{z}$), the time the air-shower core intersects with the ground ($t_{c}$), and the radius of curvature of the shower front, where the radius of curvature is only used as a free parameter if at least 5 stations are participating in the event.
Otherwise, it is parameterized from events where the radius of curvature could be determined from the fit (REF2020JINST).

Once the shower timing and wavefront properties are determined, the total shower size is estimated from a fit to the lateral distribution function (LDF) of the station signals as a function of their distance to the shower axis.
